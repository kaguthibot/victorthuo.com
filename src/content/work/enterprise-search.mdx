## Challenge

The organization's intranet had evolved organically over a decade into a fragmented ecosystem—policies lived in one system, project documentation in another, employee directories in a third, and organizational announcements in a fourth. Employees searching for information faced a hopeless choice: use a generic search that returned irrelevant results, navigate to known repositories and hope the information existed there, or ask colleagues directly. This fragmentation created real costs: redundant document creation because teams couldn't find existing resources, consistency failures because policies existed in multiple versions, and bottlenecking of institutional knowledge through informal channels.

User research revealed the severity: 62% of search attempts failed to surface relevant information on the first try, employees visited an average of 4.2 different systems per research task, and time-to-information averaged 14 minutes for questions that should have taken 2 minutes. The frustration was compounded by inconsistent search interfaces, different query syntax across systems, and lack of quality curation—both signal and noise appeared in results with no clear prioritization. We needed not just a search tool, but a fundamental reconceptualization of how the enterprise surfaced knowledge.

## Approach

We approached universal search as an experience design problem, not an engineering challenge. Rather than building an all-seeing index of everything, we designed for the 80/20: what did employees actually need to find? We conducted extensive usage analysis, finding that 80% of queries fell into five categories: policies and standards, project and team information, expert directories, historical documentation, and announcements. This insight allowed us to design a unified interface that weighted results intelligently by category while surfacing relevant context.

The interface itself was radically simplified: one search box, one query syntax, instant results with progressive refinement options. Instead of overwhelming users with thousands of results, we surfaced the most relevant resource with rich preview information and one-click navigation. For queries that needed more nuance, we provided faceted refinement filters organized around how employees actually thought about information (by team, by type, by date) rather than by backend systems.

Critically, we restructured governance: we assigned ownership to information stewards who committed to maintaining freshness and accuracy. We also redesigned how content was authored—metadata that powered search relevance was built into creation workflows, preventing the decay that plagued prior systems. We created feedback loops where employee searches that failed or showed them irrelevant results generated alerts for content teams, creating continuous refinement.

## Outcome

The universal search launched to immediate adoption—within 2 weeks, 70% of information queries moved from fragmented systems to the centralized search. Users reported discovering information in an average of 3 minutes, cutting research time by 80%. The 45% reduction in failed searches represented real value: fewer re-asks to colleagues, faster decision-making, and reduced risk of decisions made on outdated information.

The success created positive reinforcement: as employees experienced faster discovery, they used search more frequently, creating more data about what queries worked and what needed refinement. The organization invested in a dedicated content strategy role, treating search results as a customer experience to be continuously optimized. Over time, universal search became not just an efficiency tool but a change mechanism—by making information instantly accessible, the organization became more process-compliant and consistency-driven.

The experience demonstrated a design principle: sometimes the most powerful simplification is removing choice. By creating one clear, well-designed search experience, we eliminated the cognitive load of deciding where to look and freed mental energy for the actual work.
